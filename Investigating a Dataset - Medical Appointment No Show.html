Introduction¶

Background and Problem Statement¶

In the field of healthcare, no-show refers to patients who fail to show up to scheduled appointments or cancel at the last minute giving the health center no opportunity to fill the appointment slot. This problem of no-shows poses considerable challenges to the effectiveness and quality of healthcare delivery. Lacy et al. (2004) argues that patients who schedule clinic appointments and fail to keep them harm the workflow of health centers.

According to Pfeifer, no-show appointments result in a loss of time and money for the health center and disrupt the continuity of care for patients. Marbouh et al (2020) explain that no-shows can affect the patient’s health condition due to the delay in diagnosis or treatment. Macharia et al. (1992) estimated that an average of 42% of appointments become no-shows. Another study estimates that the healthcare industry loses about $150 billion to patient appointment no shows each year.

The deleterious impact of no-shows on the already resource-constrained healthcare industry has attracted the attention of researchers to investigate the causes and prescribe solutions to arrest the problem. Several studies have identified various factors influencing no-show rates. The frequently cited factors are age, gender, service quality, number of preceding appointments, appointment lead times, and waiting times (see Lacy et al., 2004; Marbouh et al., 2020). According to Glowacka et al. (2009), the reasons for no-shows include difficulty in transportation access, patients forgetting appointments, history of failed appointments, and long waiting times.

The findings of studies on no-shows, like those mentioned above, have influenced many interventions to address the problem. According to Macharia et al. (1992), the most effective intervention has been reminder calls or mailings. Other methods include providing transportation, new patient education, improved scheduling, and other forms of incentives or disincentives to discourage no-shows (see Bean and Talaga, 1992; Smith and Yawn, 1994; Gatrad, 2000). Despite efforts, Lacy et al. (2004) argues that, none of the interventions have been consistently effective. Hence, there is the need to further interrogate the issue to derive alternative insights into the causes and alternative solutions.

A Brief Review of Related Literature¶

In terms of technique and focus, the extant literature on no-shows in the healthcare industry is diverse. To investigate the topic of no-shows, some studies, such as Lacy et al. (2004), use qualitative approaches such as interviews and focus group discussions. Other studies were more quantitatively oriented, with some examining the topic using advanced data analytics and machine learning approaches (for example Mohammadi et al., 2018). Regardless of the type of research utilized, many studies agree that the problem of no-shows is detrimental to healthcare delivery, necessitating the adoption of innovative solutions to address it.

Using semi-structured interviews with 34 adult patients who came to the clinic for outpatient care, Lacy et al. (2004) investigated the perspectives of patients who were no-shows. Emotions, perceived disrespect, and a lack of knowledge of the scheduling system were the top three reasons for no-shows, according to the study's participants (patients). Other causes for no-shows were logistical challenges with keeping schedules, however, participants did not see these as major concerns. The study discovered that these participants' appointment-making was influenced by immediate symptoms and a need for self-care. Reviewing waiting times and participants' perceptions of personal respect, according to the study, could help minimize no-show rates.

Mohammadi et al. (2018) employed predictive modeling techniques to analyze patient no-shows in community health centers. The study's goal is to build and compare appointment no-show prediction models to gain a better understanding of appointment adherence in underprivileged populations. Patients' adherence to appointments differs between clinics within a healthcare system, according to the researchers. Data analytics results, according to Mohammadi et al, highlight the importance of current clinical and operational data in addressing critical operational and managerial challenges, which could lead to a reduction in no-show rates.

Marbouh et al (2020) investigate the impact of patient no-shows on the quality of healthcare services. The study uses a case study research approach to determine the rate of no-shows and their underlying causes in a radiology department at one of the region's premier hospitals. They discovered that no-shows are common owing to a variety of causes, including patient behavior, financial circumstances, environmental conditions, and scheduling policies. Several recommendations are made in the study to help reduce the frequency of patient no-shows. Patient education, the use of dynamic scheduling policies, and effective appointment reminder systems are all part of this.

Liu et al. (2022) develop an interpretable deep learning-based approach for predicting the risk of no-shows at the time when a medical appointment is first scheduled. They contend that their proposed models, which are based on neural networks and logistic regression, outperform persistent baselines. Patients' previous no-show record is found to be the biggest predictor of appointment no-show in the study. Liu et al. recommend that scheduling high-risk patients' visits during off-peak hours can help prevent no-shows.

Feature Engineering¶

In this part, we will create other variables from the existing ones. According to Mohammadi et al (2018) and Dianbo Liu et al (2022), time difference between the date of visit and date the patient had contacted the clinic is an important predictor of no-show. Furthermore, studies have found that patients' past history of no-shows is a strong predictor of present or future no-shows. In light of this information, I will create two additional features namely:

  1. Lead time: Difference between Appointment date and Schedule date (in days).
  2. Past no-show rate: the number of no-shows for a given patient prior to the last appointment, divided by the patient’s total number of appointments prior to the last appointment.

Conclusion¶

The objective of this analysis was to employ the data analysis process to investigate the reasons why patients who fail to show up to scheduled appointments or cancel at the last minute giving the health center no opportunity to fill the appointment slot. I used data on medical appointments in Brazil for the period Nov 10, 2015, to June 8, 2016. Below, I present a summary of the finding of this analysis organized by the five research questions.

Summary of Finding¶

RQ1: What demographic characteristics define patients who miss their medical appointments?

• Among the patients in the sample, 79.81% did not show up for their appointment and 65% of the patients were female.

• On average, the percentage of patients who do not show up for appointments are approximately the same among females and males i.e., 20.3% for females and 19.96% for males.

• There is a negative association between no-show and age. This suggests that no-show is more prevalent among younger patients. However, this association is weak i.e., the correlation coefficient is -0.06.

RQ2: What are the health characteristics of patients who miss their medical appointments?

• Among the patients who did not show up, 16.9% have hypertension, 6.41% have diabetes, and 3.03% deal with alcoholism.

• Of those who show up, 20.44% have hypertension, 7.38% have diabetes, and 3.04% deal with alcoholism.

• While we observe a difference in the prevalence of no-shows among patients with hypertension and diabetes, there is no difference in no-shows between alcoholics and non-alcoholics.

• Also, the analysis shows that higher levels of handcap is associated with a high percentage of no-show.

RQ3: Are patients who receive any form of intervention more likely to show up for their appointments?

• 11.55% of patients with scholarships did not show up compared to 9.39% who show up.

• Similarly, 43.85% of patients who received SMS reminders did not show up compared to 29.13% who showed up.

• This finding is interesting as it contradicts the idea that patients do not show up because of forgetfulness and that interventions such as SMS reminders will encourage showing up.

RQ4: Does waiting time and patients' history of no shows matter?

• On average, patients who did not show up have a higher lead-time (approximately 16 days) than those who show up (approximately 8 days).

• Also, patients who did not show up have higher past missed appointments than those who show up, on average.

• We observe that lead time is less than 5% among those who had an appointment on the same day of scheduling.

• Furthermore, we notice that the percentage of no-shows increases as the wait time (lead time) increases.

RQ5: What is the most important predictor of no-shows?

• Among the variables included in this analysis, the number of past missed appointments is the strongest predictor of no-shows.

• The number of past no-shows of patients has a positive and moderate correlation with no-shows i.e., a correlation coefficient of 0.58.

Limitation of the Study¶

We have achieved the objective of this analysis and answered the research questions. However, there are some drawbacks to this work. A major limitation of this analysis is that, I did not perform statistical analysis to support the relationships discovered in the analysis. As request, I cannot make inferences about the data. Also, other important variables such as income status and distance of commute to the hospitals would have provided interesting insights. Furthermore, advancing the analysis by including machine learning techniques to predicts no-shows would have improved the relevance of this project. In the immediate future, I intend to revise this project by address the aforementioned limitations.
